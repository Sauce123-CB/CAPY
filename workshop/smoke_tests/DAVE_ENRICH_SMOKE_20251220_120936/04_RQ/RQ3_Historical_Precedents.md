# Deep Research Report: Historical Precedents for Dave Inc. (NYSE: DAVE) Scenarios

## MAINLINE SCENARIO A: DOJ/CFPB Enforcement Actions Against Fintech Lenders/Earned Wage Access Providers

### Case 1: MoneyLion - Military Lending Act Violations (2022-2025)

**Nature of Violation:** MoneyLion was accused in a September 2022 CFPB lawsuit of charging more than the maximum 36% interest rate allowed by the Military Lending Act (MLA) on loans to active service members and their families [Banking Dive, Nov 2025]. MoneyLion required customers to join a membership program with monthly fees between $19.99-$29 to access low-APR installment loans, but prevented borrowers from canceling memberships until loans were paid off [Banking Dive, Nov 2025].

**Settlement Amount:** $1.75 million, reached on November 21, 2025 [U.S. News & World Report, Nov 2025].

**Stock Price Impact:** MoneyLion's share price plunged 29% the day the CFPB announced the lawsuit in September 2022, though the stock rebounded over the following week [American Banker, 2022]. The company did not admit or deny wrongdoing [Banking Dive, Nov 2025].

**Business Model Changes:** MoneyLion is now barred from extending consumer credit at rates higher than 36% to MLA-covered borrowers and cannot prevent borrowers from canceling memberships or engage in collections for past unpaid membership fees [U.S. News & World Report, Nov 2025].

**Timeline:** 38 months from lawsuit filing (September 2022) to settlement (November 2025).

**Additional Context:** MoneyLion was acquired by Gen Digital for approximately $1 billion during the pendency of the lawsuit [Banking Dive, Nov 2025].

### Case 2: Brigit - Deceptive Subscription Practices (2023)

**Nature of Violation:** The FTC took action against Brigit for misleading borrowers into believing that if they paid the $9.99 monthly membership fee, they would qualify for a cash advance of $250. In reality, many borrowers did not qualify for a cash advance in any amount [Center for Responsible Lending, 2024]. The FTC alleged that Brigit's claims that customers could cancel anytime weren't true [Salon, Jan 2025].

**Settlement Amount:** $18 million [American Banker, 2024].

**Stock Price Impact:** Not available (Brigit is a private company).

**Business Model Changes:** Required to provide accurate disclosure of advance availability and allow customers to cancel subscriptions as promised.

**APR Issues:** A CRL study found that direct-to-consumer wage advance users of companies including Brigit paid excessive, often hidden junk fees for loans with APRs as high as 367% [Center for Responsible Lending, 2024].

**Timeline:** Settlement reached in 2023; exact investigation start date not disclosed.

### Case 3: FloatMe - Discrimination and Deceptive Practices (2024)

**Nature of Violation:** On January 2, 2024, the FTC filed a complaint alleging violations of the FTC Act, ROSCA, and the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) [Consumer Finance Monitor, Jan 2024]. FloatMe allegedly violated ECOA by ignoring income derived from public assistance programs when evaluating consumers for cash advance eligibility [Consumer Finance Monitor, Jan 2024].

**Settlement Amount:** $3 million, with most distributed as consumer refunds [LendEDU, 2025].

**Stock Price Impact:** Not available (FloatMe is a private company).

**Business Model Changes:** Required to consider public assistance income in credit decisions and provide accurate disclosures about advance availability and cancellation policies.

**Timeline:** Settlement reached in 2024; investigation start date not disclosed.

### Case 4: Chime - Delayed Customer Refunds (2023)

**Nature of Violation:** The CFPB found that thousands of Chime customers waited weeks or months to recoup funds, causing financial harm. In some cases, customers were forced to seek expensive forms of credit to cover overdue bills [Banking Dive, 2023]. Chime was found in violation of the Consumer Protection Act of 2010.

**Settlement Amount:** $4.55 million total - $1.3 million minimum in customer redress plus $3.25 million penalty to CFPB's victims relief fund [Banking Dive, 2023]. Additionally, Chime paid $2.5 million in February to resolve a California DFPI investigation [Banking Dive, 2023].

**Stock Price Impact:** Not available (Chime is a private company valued at $25+ billion as of 2021).

**Business Model Changes:** Required to improve dispute resolution processes and customer service responsiveness.

**Timeline:** Not disclosed in search results.

### Case 5: LendUp - Repeat Violations of CFPB Order (2021)

**Nature of Violation:** The CFPB sued LendUp in September 2021 for continuing to engage in illegal and deceptive marketing in violation of a 2016 CFPB order [CFPB, 2021]. LendUp had been ordered in 2016 to stop misrepresenting the benefits of borrowing from the company. The company also violated the Military Lending Act (sued by CFPB in 2020) [CFPB, 2021].

**Settlement Amount:** $100,000 civil money penalty based on LendUp's demonstrated inability to pay [CFPB, 2021].

**Business Model Changes:** LendUp agreed to halt making any new loans and stop collecting on certain outstanding loans - effectively shutting down the business [CFPB, 2021].

**Stock Price Impact:** Not available (LendUp was a private company backed by Google Ventures, Andreessen Horowitz, Kleiner Perkins, PayPal, and QED Investors) [CFPB, 2021].

**Timeline:** 5 years from initial 2016 order to 2021 enforcement action that shuttered the business.

## MAINLINE SCENARIO B: Bank Partner Transitions

### Case 1: Dave Inc. - Evolve Bank to Coastal Community Bank (2025)

**Trigger:** Dave announced on March 3, 2025, that it would switch from Evolve Bank & Trust (which had been grappling with enforcement actions and other challenges) to Coastal Community Bank as its primary banking partner [Bloomberg, Mar 2025].

**Timeline:** Customers began onboarding to Coastal Community Bank in Q2 2025 [Dave Inc., Mar 2025]. On August 4, 2025, Dave amended its agreement with Coastal to manage up to $225 million in receivables [TipRanks, Aug 2025].

**Customer Disruption:** No significant customer disruption reported. The transition was structured to occur gradually with new customers onboarding to the new partner.

**Revenue Impact:** Positive impact reported. Dave reported Q2 2025 revenue of $131.7 million (64% increase) and net income of $9.1 million (42% increase), and raised 2025 guidance [GlobeNewswire, Mar 2025].

**Stock Price Impact:** The strategic partnership announcement appeared to be received positively by investors, though specific stock price data was not provided in search results.

**Strategic Benefits:** CEO Jason Wilk stated Coastal was chosen for "their customer-first mission, deep knowledge across credit and banking products, strong risk management, and our shared ambition to make a difference in communities that need it most" [Dave Inc., Mar 2025].

### Case 2: Synapse Bankruptcy - Multiple Fintechs Affected (2024)

**Trigger:** Synapse Financial Technologies, a BaaS middleware provider, filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in April 2024 after its relationships with a regional bank and fintech clients exploded. Synapse CEO alleged that Evolve Bank undercut Synapse by poaching its largest customer, Mercury [Fortune, Mar 2025].

**Affected Companies:** Yotta, Juno, Copper (teen banking startup), Mercury, Dave, Relay, and dozens of other fintechs [TechCrunch, Aug 2024].

**Timeline:** The collapse was abrupt - Synapse filed bankruptcy in April 2024 without advance warning to most fintech partners.

**Customer Disruption:** SEVERE. Over 200,000 customer accounts were frozen with approximately $85-90 million in customer funds missing/unaccounted for [BobsGuide, 2024]. For nearly three weeks, approximately 85,000 Yotta customers with joint balances of $112 million were locked out of their accounts [Fortune, Mar 2025]. Some customers received only a fraction of deposits back - one customer was told they would receive $500 out of $280,000 deposited [Yahoo Finance, 2025].

**Revenue Impact:** CATASTROPHIC for affected fintechs:
- **Yotta:** Approximately $182 million in funds moved away from Evolve Bank; company forced into crisis mode [Fortune, Mar 2025]
- **Copper:** Had to abruptly discontinue banking deposit accounts and debit cards [Banking Dive, 2024]
- **Mainvest:** Ceased operations just two months after Synapse bankruptcy [Fortune, Mar 2025]

**Stock Price Impact:** Not available for most affected companies (privately held). However, the collapse sent shockwaves through the fintech industry.

**Legal and Regulatory Actions:**
- DOJ criminal investigation launched [Fortune, Mar 2025]
- California DFPI ordered Juno to cease deposit-taking activities in August 2025 [Fortune, Mar 2025]
- FDIC proposed new rules requiring banks to maintain direct access to records of third-party entities managing customer funds [Fortune, Mar 2025]
- CFPB announced it is considering using the Civil Penalty Fund to reimburse affected individuals [BobsGuide, 2024]

**Key Lesson:** The Synapse collapse exposed critical vulnerabilities in multi-party BaaS arrangements where a middleware provider sits between fintechs and sponsor banks. FDIC insurance didn't cover losses because the banks didn't fail - the intermediary did [Yahoo Finance, 2025].

### Case 3: LendingClub Acquisition of Radius Bank (2020-2021)

**Trigger:** LendingClub proactively acquired Radius Bank to gain a stable, cheaper source of funding and obtain a national bank charter, rather than being forced to switch partners [LendingClub, Feb 2020].

**Deal Terms:** $185 million in cash and stock for Radius Bancorp (approximately $1.4 billion in assets) [CNBC, Feb 2020].

**Timeline:** Deal announced February 18, 2020; closed February 1, 2021 (12 months) [LendingClub, Feb 2021]. This was the first time a U.S. fintech company acquired a bank [Sullivan & Cromwell, 2020].

**Customer Disruption:** Minimal reported disruption. LendingClub rebranded Radius Bank to LendingClub as of July 14, 2021 [LendingClub Help, 2021].

**Revenue Impact:** Positive. LendingClub expected to save $40 million annually in bank fees and funding costs [CNBC, Feb 2020].

**Stock Price Impact:** Not detailed in search results, but the acquisition was completed as planned with regulatory approval.

**Business Model Changes:** LendingClub became "the only full-spectrum fintech marketplace bank and the first public U.S. neobank" [PR Newswire, Feb 2021]. Certain Radius businesses that didn't align with LendingClub's mission were discontinued [LendingClub Help, 2021].

## TAIL RISK - DOWNSIDE: Regulatory Prohibition Examples

### Case 1: Colorado Payday Lending Cap (2018)

**Regulatory Action:** Colorado's Proposition 111, passed in 2018, capped interest rates at 36% APR and required minimum 6-month repayment terms, effectively curtailing the payday loan industry in the state [Debt Hammer, 2025].

**Loopholes Exploited:** Payday lenders found workarounds using "alternative charge loans" not subject to the same protections. In 2023, Colorado passed HB 23-1229 to close these loopholes [Denver Post, Apr 2023].

**Further Restrictions (2023):** HB 23-1229 exercised Colorado's right to opt out of DIDMCA (federal law allowing state-chartered banks to charge rates permitted in their home state), preventing high-cost lenders like CashNetUSA and Check 'n Go from partnering with out-of-state banks to evade Colorado's rate caps [NCLC, 2023].

**Stock Impact:** Specific stock price data not available in search results for affected public companies.

**Industry Impact:** Effectively eliminated traditional payday lending in Colorado, forcing business model changes or market exits.

---

### Case 2: New York Earned Wage Access Lawsuits (April 2025)

**Regulatory Action:** On April 14, 2025, New York Attorney General Letitia James filed two separate lawsuits against earned wage access providers, alleging illegal payday lending schemes [Consumer Finance Blog, Apr 2025].

**Alleged Violations:** Companies charged fees and tips resulting in APRs far exceeding New York's civil usury cap (16%) and criminal usury cap (25%) [Consumer Finance Blog, Apr 2025].

**Stock Impact:** Not yet determined (cases ongoing).

**Timeline:** Cases filed April 2025; resolution pending.

**Broader Context:** If New York successfully prosecutes these cases, it could set a precedent for other states to treat EWA products as loans subject to usury laws, potentially prohibiting or severely restricting the business model.

---

### Case 3: China Fintech Regulatory Crackdown (2020-2023)

**Regulatory Action:** China launched a sweeping regulatory crackdown on fintech starting in late 2020, beginning with the suspension of Ant Group's $37 billion IPO just days before launch in November 2020 [CNN, Jul 2023].

**Trigger:** Jack Ma publicly criticized Chinese regulators in an October 2020 speech, 12 days before Ant's planned IPO [EqualOcean, Jan 2022].

**Scope of Crackdown:** Extended beyond fintech to broader tech sector. In April 2021, 34 tech firms were warned to "curb excesses" following antitrust review. 84 Chinese tech companies withdrew IPO applications [Anchor Capital, 2021].

**Peak-to-Trough Stock Impact:**
- The campaign wiped out more than $1 trillion in market value across prominent companies [CNN, Jan 2023]
- Five of the largest, most directly impacted Chinese companies lost $1 trillion in value between them in July 2021 [South China Morning Post, 2023]
- Alibaba was fined a record 18.2 billion yuan ($2.5 billion) for monopolistic behavior in April 2021 [CNN, Jul 2023]
- Ant Group's valuation fell 75% from its 2020 IPO valuation, as revealed by internal share buyback [EqualOcean, Jan 2022]

**Timeline:** 32 months from initial IPO suspension (November 2020) to regulators declaring the crackdown "basically over" (January 2023) [South China Morning Post, 2023].

**Ant Group Resolution:** Fined approximately $994 million (7.1 billion yuan) in July 2023 [CNN, Jul 2023]. Jack Ma ceded control of Ant Group [TechCrunch, Jul 2023].

**Key Lesson:** Government criticism of fintech business models, even in democratic countries, can have severe market consequences. The China example represents an extreme case of regulatory prohibition that effectively reshaped the fintech industry.

### Case 4: CFPB Interpretive Rule on Earned Wage Access (2024-2025)

**Regulatory Action:** In January 2025, the CFPB rescinded an advisory opinion from the first Trump Administration that held certain EWA products were not "credit" under Regulation Z [ABA Banking Journal, Jan 2025]. In July 2024, the CFPB proposed an interpretive rule clarifying that EWA products are loans and that tips/expedite fees are finance charges under TILA [Center for Responsible Lending, Jul 2024].

**Potential Impact:** If finalized, would require EWA providers to:
- Disclose true APRs (which can reach 367% to 580% per CRL study) [Center for Responsible Lending, 2024]
- Comply with Truth in Lending Act requirements
- Potentially face state usury caps

**Industry Response:** Mixed. Some states (Nevada, Kansas, Wisconsin, Missouri, South Carolina) enacted laws favorable to EWA services, while others (California, New York) increased scrutiny [NCLC, 2025].

**Stock Impact:** Pending rule finalization. Current political uncertainty around CFPB's future under new administration [U.S. News, Nov 2025].

**Timeline:** Proposed July 2024; final rule timing uncertain given administration change.

## TAIL RISK - UPSIDE: Major Bank Acquisitions of Fintech Neobanks

### Case 1: Goldman Sachs Acquisition of GreenSky (2021-2023) - FAILED

**Acquisition Details:** Goldman Sachs announced on September 15, 2021, an agreement to acquire GreenSky, the largest fintech platform for home improvement consumer loan originations, in an all-stock transaction initially valued at $2.24 billion [Goldman Sachs, Sep 2021].

**Closing Terms:** By the time Goldman finalized the deal in March 2022, the value had dropped to $1.73 billion [Yahoo Finance, Oct 2023].

**Acquisition Multiples:** Not disclosed in terms of EV/Revenue or EV/User, but the deal represented a significant premium for a point-of-sale lending platform.

**Outcome:** FAILURE. Goldman sold GreenSky in October 2023 to a Sixth Street-led consortium for approximately $500 million - a 71% loss from the $1.73 billion purchase price [PYMNTS, Oct 2023]. Goldman disclosed a $504 million write-down in July 2023 [Yahoo Finance, Oct 2023].

**Timeline:** Owned for 19 months (March 2022 - October 2023).

**Key Lesson:** Not all major bank acquisitions of fintechs succeed. This represents a cautionary tale rather than an upside scenario for Dave.

## SYNTHESIS AND KEY IMPLICATIONS FOR DAVE INC.

### Scenario A: Regulatory Enforcement - Key Patterns

1. **Settlement Amounts vs. Company Size:** Range from $100K (LendUp, ability to pay limited) to $18M (Brigit). MoneyLion's $1.75M settlement represented approximately 0.175% of its $1 billion acquisition value.

2. **Stock Impact Timeline:** MoneyLion's 29% single-day drop (then partial recovery) suggests acute but potentially recoverable impacts if the company remains viable.

3. **Business Model Restrictions:** Most common requirements include APR caps (36% for MLA), improved disclosures, and prohibition of coercive practices. LendUp represents worst case - complete business shutdown.

4. **Investigation Duration:** Ranges from months to multiple years. MoneyLion's case lasted 38 months from lawsuit to settlement.

5. **Current Regulatory Environment:** CFPB enforcement activity is winding down under new administration as of late 2025, with only 12 active litigation cases remaining [U.S. News, Nov 2025]. However, state AGs (particularly New York) are increasing activity.

### Scenario B: Bank Partner Transitions - Key Patterns

1. **Proactive vs. Forced Transitions:**
   - **Proactive** (Dave, LendingClub, SoFi): Minimal disruption, positive revenue impact, often involves bank acquisition
   - **Forced** (Synapse collapse): Catastrophic customer and revenue impact

2. **Timeline Range:**
   - **Successful:** 3-12 months with gradual customer migration
   - **Crisis:** Immediate impact (Synapse - overnight account freezes)

3. **Customer Disruption Minimization:** Successful transitions feature gradual onboarding, clear communication, and maintaining service continuity. Chime's dual-partner model offers redundancy.

4. **Revenue Impact:** Successful transitions often improve economics (LendingClub saved $40M annually; Dave posted 64% revenue growth during transition).

5. **Critical Risk:** Middleware dependency. Synapse's collapse demonstrates catastrophic risk when a non-bank intermediary sits between fintech and sponsor bank. Direct bank relationships are significantly safer.

### Downside Tail Risk: Regulatory Prohibition - Key Patterns

1. **Peak-to-Trough Impact:** China example shows extreme case of 75% valuation decline (Ant Group) and $1 trillion wiped from market caps.

2. **State-Level Prohibition:** Colorado's 36% APR cap effectively eliminated traditional payday lending. New York's April 2025 lawsuits treating EWA as loans subject to usury caps (16-25%) could prohibit high-cost EWA models if successful.

3. **Timeline:** Regulatory prohibition can occur rapidly (China - overnight IPO cancellation) or gradually (Colorado - 2018 law, 2023 loophole closure, ongoing enforcement).

4. **Loophole Exploitation:** Industry consistently finds workarounds (alternative charge loans, "tips" vs. fees), requiring repeated regulatory action.

5. **Current CFPB Proposed Rule:** If finalized, could require Dave to disclose true APRs and comply with TILA, potentially triggering state usury caps. However, political uncertainty around CFPB's future creates ambiguity.

### Upside Tail Risk: Bank Acquisition - Key Patterns

1. **Acquisition Multiples (2025):**
   - **North America Average:** 6.4x EV/Revenue
   - **Global Average:** 4.4x EV/Revenue
   - Significant decline from 5-year average of 5.2x

2. **Premium to Stock Price:** Data limited, but Goldman-GreenSky initially valued at $2.24B and InstaMed at $500M+ suggest substantial premiums for strategic assets.

3. **Major Bank Acquisition Appetite:** VERY LIMITED. Top banks constitute less than 1% of fintech acquirers over past decade [FinTech Magazine, 2025]. Banks show more interest in acquiring other banks (PNC-BBVA) or specific infrastructure (JPMorgan-InstaMed) than consumer-facing neobanks.

4. **Reverse Acquisitions More Common:** Fintechs acquiring banks (LendingClub-Radius $185M, SoFi-Golden Pacific, Enova-Grasshopper $369M) more prevalent than banks acquiring fintechs.

5. **Success Rate:** Mixed. Goldman-GreenSky failed spectacularly (71% loss in 19 months), while UniCredit-Aion and JPMorgan-InstaMed appear more successful.

6. **Realistic Upside for Dave:** Given limited major bank appetite for neobank acquisitions and Dave's EWA-focused model (which faces regulatory scrutiny), acquisition by a major bank appears UNLIKELY in near term. More plausible scenarios include:
   - Acquisition by another fintech seeking to add EWA capabilities
   - Private equity acquisition (similar to Sixth Street buying GreenSky)
   - Acquisition of a bank BY Dave (similar to LendingClub, SoFi, Enova)
